Thinking about disabilities

On average, around 8% to 12% of college students are officially registered with their school's disability services office, but that's just the tip of the iceberg. A 2022 NCES survey noted that only a third (37%) of students with disabilities informed their college.

Many faculty and staff members use accommodations and experience disabilities. Students, faculty, and staff are not legally required to inform their colleges of their disabilities and many choose not to do so. Here are some reasons that disabilities may be underreported on campus:

  • Some people may not identify as disabled.
  • Others may not want to register with their local center.
  • Many people don't know that disability accommodations exist or apply to them.
  • Some people prefer not to talk about their disability.
  • Many people prefer to avoid bureaucracy by managing disabilities on their own.

Contemporary disability models

Accessibility is often discussed in the context of disability. Refer to the following list of different thought-models that can be used to describe disability. Each of these models of disability has limitations and do not represent a complete understanding of what it means to be disabled, but they have all shaped the way society views and talks about both disability and accessibility.

Medical Model
Under the medical model, people often classify disabilities by individual function and see disability as an individual problem to be fixed or cured by medical professionals. Disabilities tend to be categorized into areas of the body in which function is affected. Examples of those categories:
  • Visual (blindness, low vision, color blindness)
  • Auditory (deafness, hard of hearing)
  • Motor (paralysis, cerebral palsy, missing/damaged limbs)
  • Cognitive (learning disabilities, traumatic brain injury, dyslexia)
  • Seizure (epilepsy)
  • Mental health (depression, anxiety, panic attacks)
The Medical Model focuses on an accommodations approach, which relies on modifying or adjusting the way things are usually done to enable an individual with a disability to participate. It's reactive to the needs of the individual rather than being proactive in creating an environment in which everyone is able to participate, without accommodations.
Social Model
The social model of disability began to emerge in the 1970s, when changes to societal structure and educational inclusion led to people with disabilities gaining platforms to publicly reflect on their own realities.
In the social model, disability is defined by society's ideas about what makes bodies and minds valuable. These ideas lead to decisions about physical spaces, technologies, laws, and policies, which often exclude and discriminate against people whose minds and bodies function differently.
The Social Model focuses on how society as a whole can change so everyone can participate fully.
Outdated disability models
Moral Model
This model sees disability as something that has happened to a person, possibly because of that person's (or that person's family's) history, carelessness or societally unacceptable actions - not doing enough of the right things.
Charity Model
In this model, a disability is a tragedy. People with disabilities should be given charity and "be taken care of", often away from the public eye.
Rehabilitation Model
This model posits that people with disabilities should work hard to appear as normal as possible, regardless of the (social, economic, and personal labor) cost.

Note: It may feel uncomfortable to read about the three outdated models listed above, because each displays a direct set of assumptions and biases about people with disabilities. But reflect on how these models may still have influence. Have you ever heard someone in a wheelchair described as "inspiring"? Have you ever heard someone blame a person with depression for "being spoiled"? These statements and attitudes are reflective of some of these outdated models.
