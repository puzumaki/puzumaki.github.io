Skill 5: practice

Lists are powerful for digital content. They aid in content scanning and generally improve readability. Use bulleted lists where appropriate.

Consider how to use lists to make the content easier to read in the following blocks of text.

Exercise 1

Lists can provide a break in the document flow and encourage users to stick with the content. Use lists to present key terms and concepts, organize information into meaningful chunks, convey sequencing necessary for processes and procedures, create links to additional information, and write parallel list items that have the same structure. This will help users comprehend text more quickly, reducing reader fatigue resulting from trying to comprehend dense or complex paragraphs.

Possible answer

Lists can provide a break in the document flow and encourage users to stick with the content. Use lists to:

  • present key terms and concepts,
  • organize information into meaningful chunks,
  • convey sequencing necessary for processes and procedures,
  • create links to additional information, and
  • write parallel list items that have the same structure.

This will help users comprehend text more quickly, reducing reader fatigue resulting from trying to comprehend dense or complex paragraphs.

Exercise 2

After four decades of publishing, King’s stories have been a rite of passage for generations. For many, they’re parts of growing up, and stealing through his pages at a young age often felt like an act of daring and defiance. The stories that were part of that coming-of-age tend to rank a little higher. The top five books would be The Shining, giving us a Jack Torrance in the isolated Overlook Hotel; then The Dead Zone where Johnny Smith wakes up from a coma with supernatural powers; in third is It, known for its shape-shifting killer clown; The Stand is next with its exploration of what happens when strictures of society vanish forever; and in first place is Pet Sematary, which also is the book King likes the least, describing the grief of lost loved ones.

Possible answer

After four decades of publishing, King’s stories have been a rite of passage for generations. For many, they’re parts of growing up, and stealing through his pages at a young age often felt like an act of daring and defiance. The stories that were part of that coming-of-age tend to rank a little higher. The top five books would be:

  1. The Shining, giving us a Jack Torrance in the isolated Overlook Hotel.
  2. The Dead Zone where Johnny Smith wakes up from a coma with supernatural powers.
  3. It, known for its shape-shifting killer clown.
  4. The Stand explores what happens when strictures of society vanish forever.
  5. Pet Sematary, which also is the book King likes the least, describing the grief of lost loved ones.
