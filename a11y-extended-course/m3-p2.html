Etiquette is showing respect

“...we all need to acknowledge the small and big ways we participate in making it harder for someone else to exist.”

Mia Mingus, Creating Change Award Acceptance Speech, 2008

Respect

  • Recognize that disabled people are inherently worthwhile.
  • Listen to disabled people’s stories, experiences, and perspectives.
  • Understand that having a disability does not make our lives any more inspirational, pitiful or tragic than yours. Our disabilities are ordinary and familiar parts of who we are.
  • Ask (and listen to the response) before you attempt to help. What you assume is helpful may not be. Start with a friendly, non-intrusive question: “May I assist you?” or “Can I get that for you?” Your offer may not be needed or accepted. We are the experts about our own lives and what we need.
  • Don't ask intrusive questions, however well-intentioned. Because of how disabled people are separated from society, many of us deal with daily curiosity about our bodies and lives. This can be irritating, exhausting, and demeaning. Once you get to know someone, ask if you may ask a private question (and respect the response) before asking about bodies, diagnoses, experiences, medical care, sexuality, or other private topics.
  • Challenge your own assumptions. Many people have disabilities that might not be apparent to you. Everyone has a right to request and use the accommodations they need without being criticized or questioned.
  • Ignore service animals while they’re working. Don’t pet, feed, or interact with them.
  • Keep the power with the disabled person.

Adapted by Jay Wilson and Linda Wolford, Disability Resource Center, University of MN from: a community resource - created by disability rights activists (as published by Eli Clare)

Give respect to people and their identities

Everyone wants a sense of belonging, and giving people respect is the first step. For people with disabilities:

  • Remember disability is an intersecting identity (learn more with Mia Mingus' Disability justice is simply another term for love)
  • Ask if they prefer person first or disability first language if the situation that calls for that knowledge
  • Ask people what accessibility is for them (e.g. captions turned on in video calls, longer breaks in meetings or sessions)

Basic etiquette

Ask before you help

Just because someone has a disability, don’t assume they need help. If the setting is accessible, people with disabilities can usually get around.

Treat people with disabilities as independent people. A person with a disability will oftentimes communicate when they need help. And if they do want help, ask how before you act.

Be sensitive about physical contact

Some people with disabilities depend on their arms for balance. Grabbing them, even if your intention is to assist, could knock them off balance. Avoid touching them or their wheelchair, scooter, or cane. People with disabilities’ equipment is a part of their personal space.

Think before you speak

Always speak directly to the person with a disability, not to their companion, aide, or sign language interpreter. Talk as you would with anyone else. Respect their privacy.

Don’t make assumptions

People with disabilities are the best judge of what they can or cannot do. Don't make decisions for them about participating in any activity. Do not exclude people because of a presumption about their limitations.

Respond graciously to requests

When people who have disabilities ask for an accommodation, it is not a complaint. It shows they feel comfortable enough to ask for what they need.

This is the Independence Australia A - Z of Disability Etiquette.

  • A - Ask before you help, it's not always wanted.
  • B - Be patient with how others communicate.
  • C - Communicate naturally and with expressions.
  • D - Don't ask intrusive questions.
  • E - Communicate at eye level.
  • F - Focus on the person not their disability.
  • G - Don't use patronizing gestures like patting a head or shoulder.
  • H - Host events in accessible spaces.
  • I - Always respect a person's choices and independence.
  • J - Avoid being judgmental.
  • K - Keep paths and accessible car parks clear of barriers
  • L - Leave accessible toilets for people with a disability.
  • M - Don't move someone's wheelchair or assistive devices without permission.
  • N - Never assume someone's access needs; ask if you're not sure.
  • O - Avoid offensive language about disability.
  • P - Do not pat a service dog at work.
  • Q - Be committed to increasing quality of life for people with disabilities.
  • R - Research disability and accessibility to be a better ally.
  • S - Speak directly to the person with the disability.
  • T - Give up your seat on public transport if someone else needs it.
  • U - Never pretend to understand what a person is saying, it's okay to ask for clarification.
  • V - Value the work of people with disability and encourage their input to society.
  • W - Be willing to be flexible.
  • X - See a person's x factor and not their limitations.
  • Y - Introduce yourself as you would with anyone else.
  • Z - Zip it! Not everyone wants to talk about their disability.
