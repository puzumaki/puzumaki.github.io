Understand ableist microaggressions

Content warning: Some examples on the page may trigger trauma, anxiety, or other harms. For this reason, all examples are optional and closed unless manually opened.

Ableist microaggressions are verbal or non-verbal behaviors, which can be intentional or unintentional, that discriminate against or are exclusive of people with physical or developmental disabilities.

Lett defines more deeply:

When ableism is combined with subtle or brief acts of discrimination, ableist microaggressions are formed. [...] Perpetrators of ableist microaggressions are often unaware of the detrimental impact of their behaviour and usually believe that their actions are helpful.

Microaggressions are subtle but offensive comments or actions that are unintentional and reinforce a prior mental image. They are typically directed at marginalized groups and oftentimes go uncorrected, allowing them to occur regularly.

Examples of microaggressions

Verbal examples

Here are a few ableist interactions that people with disabilities shared:

  • A stranger asked me personal questions based on my disability status.
  • I experienced someone telling me my disability status is more or less severe than I think it is.
  • I have been told people with my disability status are burdensome.
  • I was asked for proof of my disability status.
  • I was expected to educate others on my disability status.
  • I was given unsolicited encouragement based on my disability status.
  • Someone expressed pity at people who share my disability status.
  • Someone changed how they spoke to me based on my disability status.

Ableist microaggressions may include telling someone that they speak very well for a Deaf person, asking everyone to stand for an activity (with the assumption that everyone can stand), making a joke about how fast someone can go in their wheelchair, talking to someone's companion instead of directly to them, or telling someone they are “lucky” that they are able to bring their service dog with them.

Behavioral examples

Microaggressions go beyond slang terms. Some behavioral microaggressions include:

  • Crouching down with your hands on your knees to talk to someone in a wheelchair
  • Making assumptions that you know what someone's limitations might be, because you have seen that disability before
  • Telling someone “You're so brave” just for existing in the same space as you

The best way to address ableism in the workplace is to operationalize inclusive hiring practices, educate your employees on biases and microaggressions, and ensure that physical office spaces are accessible.

Ableist microaggressions in the university

Findings from “Impact of ableist microaggressions on university students with self-identified disabilities”:

  • University students with disabilities experience both blatant and subtle forms of discrimination on campus.
  • The research found that experiencing disability-related discrimination negatively impacts beliefs about academic performance as well as mental health symptoms.
  • When academic institutions respond inadequately to discrimination, mental health symptoms are worse.
  • Providing good support did not seem to lessen mental health symptoms.
  • We recommend that academic institutions focus on prevention of disability-related discrimination rather than providing support only after discrimination happens.

The report concluded that:

…academic institutions should strive to ensure campus environments are accessible to all students and that the campus community is educated on disability-related discrimination. Furthermore, it is imperative that university institutions play an active role in combating discrimination at all levels. [...] institutional environments that promote or respond ineffectively to disability-related discrimination are detrimental to the well-being of students, and offering support services may not buffer this effect. We recommend that universities focus on prevention.
