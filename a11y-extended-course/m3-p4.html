Interaction access with examples

Remember, the best skill to bring to human interaction is to use active listening (10 minute read).

Access

  • Work to create accessibility in your community, workplace, and place of worship. Ramps and elevators are vital, but access doesn't stop there. Ways to start to increase access include working to:
    • Ensure that people with mobility aids and personal assistants can use the bathrooms.
    • Remove scented products and other triggers for people with respiratory disabilities and chemical sensitivities, working toward creating scent-free spaces.
    • Offer printed materials in alternative formats, such as Braille, large print, & audio.
    • Provide professional sign language interpretation and real time captioning.
    • Caption and audio describe video or audio content.
  • Access means creating an inclusive welcoming space for all community members:
    • Choose locations for community events near public transportation.
    • Offer sliding scales at events that cost money.
  • Plan events well in advance, with a contact person for questions about accessibility, location, cost, and other access details.
  • Take your lead from disabled people. We know what works best for us.
  • Be creative and seek resources. Don't use lack of money as an excuse not to create access.
  • Understand that no single accommodation will work for all disabled people. One solution doesn't fit all, but increased access does benefit everyone.

Adapted by Jay Wilson and Linda Wolford, Disability Resource Center, University of MN from: a community resource - created by disability rights activists (as published by Eli Clare)

For people with auditory disabilities

  • Wave or tap someone on the shoulder to get attention.
  • Write notes (where appropriate, use ASL, interpreter, captionist).
  • Talk directly to the person, not the interpreter.
  • Speak in a normal speed and tone unless asked to do otherwise.
  • Do not stand in front of a light source, if possible.
  • Be expressive with body language, gestures, and facial expressions.
  • Turn on closed captions by default in online meetings.

For people in wheelchairs

  • For longer interactions, position yourself in such a way that you are each able to make eye contact without straining your neck, e.g. by sitting down or standing slightly further back
  • Always ask before giving assistance, and accept “no” as an answer.
  • Remember a wheelchair is part of the person's personal space. Do not touch, lean on, or push a chair without permission.
  • Reconfigure space to make clearance for mobility devices or service animals. Consider using alternative space if needed.

For people with speech and language disabilities

  • Allow time for the person to respond.
  • Avoid the urge to interrupt or complete a sentence for the person.
  • Ask for repetition if you do not understand what the person said.
  • Use a variety of communication methods such as writing notes, emailing, or other technological options.
  • Be patient.

For people with learning disabilities or ADHD

  • Use multiple methods of delivering information, such as aural, visual, or in summarizing bullet points.
  • Offer to follow up via email. Write succinctly and begin with any action items.
  • Recognize that unconventional responses may be influenced by individual processing. Explore to understand.
  • Minimize environmental distractions such as screensavers and background noise.
  • Encourage and support the use of fidget or other stimming objects.

Learn more: Check out Making Content Usable for People with Cognitive and Learning Disabilities (6 to 7 hours) from the W3C for more recommendations to convey information.

For people with blindness or visual impairments

  • Understand that legally blind people may have some vision.
  • Provide very explicit directions if asked, and avoid visual and imprecise terms such as “over there” or “turn that way.” Instead, be explicit, such as “turn left and in 20 paces is the door.”
  • Provide any information you would present visually in an accessible format in advance.
  • Offer your arm or elbow when leading someone who is blind.
  • Do not pet, feed, or distract a service animal without permission.

For people with cognitive disabilities

  • Be very clear and specific in your language. Sarcasm and subtle humor may be missed.
  • Present instructions in a clear, easy to understand way.
  • Present oral information at a measured pace, using pauses as appropriate to facilitate time to process or ask questions.
  • Offer process cues to help with transitions, such as “we have five minutes left until our meeting is done.”
  • Reinforce information in multiple formats.
