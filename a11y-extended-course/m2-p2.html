Defining implicit bias

Understanding implicit bias and why it matters sets the foundation for meaningful pursuit of diversity, equity, inclusion, accessibility, and justice (DEIAJ). Implicit bias, a type of social bias, refers to the cultural attitudes or stereotypes that affect everyone's understanding, actions, and decisions in an unconscious way.

Bias
Although it's often used in a negative context, the word bias simply means an evaluation or belief. That means, it's possible to have a bias that is favorable or unfavorable. In other words — people can have a bias toward an object, person, or concept that is positive or negative.
Bias, in and of itself, is a neutral term. For example, take these three colors: someone could say that they are biased toward red, or biased against blue. Or maybe they're neutral toward yellow. All of those statements could be true. And, most people would agree that a person's preferences for red over yellow or blue don't make them a bad person. They are simply preferences, and that person probably did nothing to consciously control those preferences
Implicit bias
Like “bias”, the term “implicit” in and of itself isn't a good or bad thing; people rely on implicit processes to move efficiently throughout the world. By definition, implicit bias is nothing more than a person's evaluations or beliefs, whether positive or negative, that can exist without people realizing it.

Why discuss hidden biases if they aren't necessarily bad? Because it is possible to form implicit evaluations based on inaccurate information or stereotypes of people, objects, and ideas. There's also evidence that implicit biases can impact decisions, perceptions, and behaviors, making it more difficult to live up to values of equity and fairness.

Key point: The actions and decisions resulting from implicit biases can create real world barriers to equity and opportunity. Practical applications are why it's so important to learn about implicit bias, particularly those biases that don't align with intentions, or explicit beliefs.
