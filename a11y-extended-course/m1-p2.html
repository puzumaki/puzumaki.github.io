Accessibility versus accommodation

When engaging in discussions about accessibility, sometimes the word accommodation is mentioned. What is an accommodation, and what is the difference between accessibility and accommodation?

Accessibility
Provides equal access for everyone by design
Accommodation
Provides additional support for needs of people with disabilities that have not been met

In other words, accessibility is proactive; accommodation is reactive.

When a slide deck, course site, or document has been created in an inaccessible way, people may need accommodations in order to access it. At your university, the staff of your disability resource center are constantly meeting accommodation requests from students, faculty, and staff. If those materials had been created with accessibility in mind from the start, then they might never need to be "retrofitted".

After completing this course, consider learning more about creating accessible materials through your university's accessibility efforts. These trainings may help you adjust the materials you already have, but just as importantly, they will teach you the skills you need in order to create materials accessibly from the start. It will take you time to learn these skills, but once you do, you will be able to reach more people while saving time and effort.

Inaccessible vs. accommodations vs. accessible design

You may have encountered this image before in other contexts. It applies to accessibility and accommodation too:

  • Inaccessible materials leave people out.
  • Accommodations take extra work to create; they give access to whoever is present at the moment.
  • Accessible design will give access to present and future users. It may not require extra work from you in the future.

Image from the University of Minnesota includes 3 different graphics:

  • Inaccessible design excludes people. (3 people outside a wooden fence, the first 2 are tall enough to see over it, the third cannot)
  • Accommodations give access to whoever is there at the moment. They often require extra work. (3 people outside a wooden fence, the first can see without a box, the second person can see over the fence with one box, and the third has two boxes to see over the fence)
  • Accessible design means that current and future viewers will have access, without extra work. (3 people outside a chain link fence that two can see over and the third can see through)
